Scraping and analyzing dark net market platforms¶

Nick Janetos

December 9th, 2015

The 'dark net' refers to web servers which you can't find by searching on Google, either because they're not indexed, or because they hide behind some layer of security, such as the Tor network. It famously consists of new electronic marketplaces on which illicit goods are traded. For an overview, see for example two news stories on recent events.

  • "International Raids Target Sites Selling Contraband on the Dark Web", The New York Times, November 2014
  • "A 50m Drug And Gun Dark Web Market Just Disappeared And Millions In Bitcoin With It", Forbes, March 2015

I became interested in these market platforms last year, and I noticed that there didn't seem to be much data available on the scope or inner workings of these market platforms. In September 2014 I started scraping data from the most famous of the market platforms, the Silk Road. Three months later the site was seized by the FBI and the owner arrested, by then I was scraping multiple market platforms. In the year since I've scraped a total of nine market platforms, which have variously either exited the market due to the actions of law enforcement, or disappeared along with the assets of market participants.

In this document I give a brief overview of how these markets work and how I scrape them. I show some of the data which is available, and I list some broad research questions I think are interesting.

Scraping the dark net¶

All of these market platforms are accessible only through the Tor network. The Tor network is a network of servers which 'launder' web traffic, bouncing it around between themselves in order to anonymize the source of the traffic. Once an individual can access the Tor network, the marketplaces can be visited and looks similar to a marketplace like amazon.com. For example, this is what the front page of the Agora marketplace, formally one of the largest marketplaces, looked like:

I scraped these sites at (roughly) weekly intervals, then, I run the raw HTML source through scripts which extract price and listing information. The github page for the scraper is here. The github page for the processing scripts is here.

General research questions¶

  1. These marketplaces seem like a well-suited environment for empirically investigating the effects of reputation in online marketplaces, because unlike a market platform such as amazon.com, in which the buyer often has some means for recourse and the stakes are often low, here there is no means for recourse and the stakes are high (i.e., jail-time or health problems), so that reputation is more important. To my knowledge there are no compelling, tractable models of reputation suitable for empirical application, at the same time one of my research interests involves tractable models of dynamic learning. I have the first attempts at such a model of reputation here. Related questions are:
    1. How effective are reputation mechanisms in such markets at preventing cheating?
    2. How are these market platforms affected by the closure of other market platforms (through legal action or otherwise).
  2. Currently the only source of data on drug prices comes from STRIDE, which lists the prices the DEA pays for drugs which they sieze in drug busts. There is some controversy about the interpretation of these prices. How does the data from the DEA compare to the prices available on the deep web?
  3. Many of these marketplaces act as intermediaries, holding funds in escrow until a deal is complete to facilitate trust. Historically, if the sum total of these escrow funds grows too large, the market platform has disappeared along with the escrow funds. Two of the market platforms instead used a 'three party authentication' system which allows the market platform to hold the funds in escrow, but 'lock' the funds until two out of the three parties involved agree to release them. Did these different trust enforcement mechanisms affect prices and success of the market platforms?

Data description / statistics¶

The raw HTML is available for all of the websites, but only the Agora marketplace has the all the scripts written to fully extract the data in a machine-readable form. In this section, I describe what this data looks like.

Each market is run in roughly the same way. Sellers can post and set the price for listings. Buyers can purchase from sellers just like on amazon.com (right down to the use of a 'shopping cart'). Then, they can leave reviews once they receive the finished product. Here is a table with the first 10 listings in the dataset:

The columns Title, Category, Vendor, and Units are self-explanatory. The columns for Amount and Quality list the amount of the good (i.e., 30 mg) and the number of that good offered for sale in a listing. The columns Ships from and Ships to store any special information about the location the listing ships from or the location(s) it ships to.

Each listing may be scraped on multiple dates. On each date, the price of the listing is noted, as well as other seller characteristics, and the list of reviews left on that page since the last time it was scraped. These two features (price/seller characteristics and reviews) are stored separately. Here is a sample of 10 rows from the price dataset:
